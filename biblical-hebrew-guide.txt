# BIBLICAL HEBREW INSTRUCTION MANUAL FOR AI LANGUAGE MODELS

## Purpose & Scope

This document is a comprehensive reference for parsing, translating, and analyzing Biblical Hebrew (BH) — specifically the Hebrew of the Torah (Pentateuch/Chumash). It is designed so that an AI language model can produce accurate, contextually sensitive readings of Torah text at the level of a competent Hebrew student with access to traditional commentaries.

**Cardinal rule:** Biblical Hebrew is NOT Modern Hebrew. Vocabulary overlaps significantly, but grammar, syntax, semantic ranges, and idiom differ substantially. Never default to Modern Hebrew meanings when parsing Torah text.

**Methodology:** Peshat-first. The plain contextual meaning of the text takes priority. When multiple readings are grammatically possible, present all with evidence. Flag when a reading is peshat vs. derash (homiletical).

# PART ONE: THE WRITING SYSTEM

## 1.1 The Consonantal Text (Ketiv)

The Torah scroll contains only consonants (plus matres lectionis — see below). The vowel points (nikkud) were added by the Masoretes (c. 6th–10th century CE, Tiberias). This means:

- The **consonantal text** is older and more authoritative than the vowel pointing
- Occasionally the Masoretes' vowel choice reflects an interpretive decision, not the only possible reading
- The Ketiv (כתיב, "what is written") vs. Qere (קרי, "what is read") system preserves cases where the Masoretes read differently from what is written

**Matres Lectionis** (אמות קריאה): Consonant letters used to indicate vowels:
- ו can indicate /o/ or /u/
- י can indicate /i/ or /e/
- ה at word-end can indicate /a/, /e/, or /o/
- א occasionally indicates /a/ or /e/

These are NOT vowel points — they are part of the consonantal text itself.

## 1.2 The Vowel System (Nikkud)

### Full Vowels

| Name | Symbol | Sound | Class |
|------|--------|-------|-------|
| Qamats (גדול) | ָ | /a/ | A-class long |
| Patach | ַ | /a/ | A-class short |
| Tsere | ֵ | /e/ | E-class long |
| Segol | ֶ | /e/ | E-class short |
| Chiriq (male) | ִי | /i/ | I-class long |
| Chiriq (chaser) | ִ | /i/ | I-class short |
| Cholem (male) | וֹ | /o/ | O-class long |
| Cholem (chaser) | ֹ | /o/ | O-class short |
| Qamats Qatan | ָ | /o/ | O-class short |
| Shuruq | וּ | /u/ | U-class long |
| Qubbuts | ֻ | /u/ | U-class short |

### Critical: Qamats Gadol vs. Qamats Qatan

These look identical ( ָ ) but represent different vowels:
- **Qamats Gadol** = /a/ (the common one)
- **Qamats Qatan** = /o/ (appears in closed, unaccented syllables)

Example: כָּל (kol, "all/every") — this is qamats qatan, /o/, not /a/.
Compare: כָּלָה (kalah, "bride") — first qamats is qatan /o/ → wait, no. Actually כָּל has qamats qatan because the syllable is closed (ends in consonant) and unaccented in certain positions. Recognition requires syllable analysis.

**Rule for identifying Qamats Qatan:**
1. The syllable must be **closed** (CVC pattern — consonant-vowel-consonant)
2. The syllable must be **unaccented** (no ta'am/accent mark)
3. Common words: כָּל (kol), חָכְמָה (chokh-mah — first qamats is qatan), צָהֳרָיִם (tso-ho-ra-yim)

### Half-Vowels (Reduced/Chataf Vowels)

| Name | Symbol | Sound |
|------|--------|-------|
| Shva Na (vocal) | ְ | very brief /ə/ |
| Shva Nach (silent) | ְ | ∅ (closes syllable) |
| Chataf Patach | ֲ | brief /a/ |
| Chataf Segol | ֱ | brief /e/ |
| Chataf Qamats | ֳ | brief /o/ |

### Shva Na vs. Shva Nach — Critical Distinction

Both are written identically ( ְ ). Determination rules:

**Shva is NA (vocal/pronounced) when:**
1. It opens a word: בְּרֵאשִׁית (bə-re-shit)
2. It follows another shva (the second of two consecutive shvas): יִשְׁמְרוּ (yish-mə-ru) — first shva is nach, second is na
3. It follows a long vowel: שׁוֹמְרִים (sho-mə-rim)
4. It is under a letter with dagesh chazaq: הַמְּלָכִים (ham-mə-la-khim)

**Shva is NACH (silent/closes syllable) when:**
1. It ends a word: דָּבַרְתְּ (da-vart) — both final shvas are nach
2. It is the first of two consecutive shvas mid-word: יִשְׁמְרוּ (yish-mə-ru) — first is nach
3. It follows a short vowel in a closed syllable: מַלְכִּי (mal-ki)

## 1.3 The Dagesh

### Dagesh Qal (Lene) — BeGaD KeFaT letters only
Applies to: בּ גּ דּ כּ פּ תּ
- **With dagesh** = hard/plosive pronunciation
- **Without dagesh** = soft/fricative pronunciation (historically; in modern pronunciation only ב/כ/פ maintain the distinction)

### Dagesh Chazaq (Forte) — Any letter except gutturals (א ה ח ע) and ר
- Indicates **gemination** (doubling of the consonant)
- The letter closes the preceding syllable AND opens the next
- Example: הַמֶּלֶךְ (ham-me-lekh) — the dagesh in מ means the word is syllabified as ham-me-lekh, not ha-me-lekh

**Critical for parsing:** Dagesh chazaq after the definite article (הַ) compensates for the assimilated nun or indicates the article itself. Missing dagesh in gutturals often causes **compensatory lengthening** of the preceding vowel.

## 1.4 The Cantillation Marks (Te'amim / Trop)

These are NOT merely musical notation. They function as **syntactic markers** — essentially, the ancient equivalent of punctuation and phrase-grouping.

### Disjunctive Accents (Separators) — Major Ones

| Name | Function | Strength |
|------|----------|----------|
| Sof Pasuq (׃) | End of verse | Terminal |
| Atnach (֑) | Major mid-verse pause | Emperor |
| Zaqef Qaton (֔) | Secondary pause | King |
| Zaqef Gadol (֕) | Secondary pause (variant) | King |
| Tipcha (֖) | Pause before Sof Pasuq/Atnach | Duke |
| Segolta (֒) | Strong disjunction | Duke |
| Revia (֗) | Medium disjunction | Duke |

### Conjunctive Accents (Connectors) — Major Ones

| Name | Function |
|------|----------|
| Munach (֣) | Connects to following word |
| Mercha (֥) | Connects to following word |
| Mahpach (֤) | Connects to following word |
| Darga (֧) | Connects to following word |

### Why This Matters for Translation

The te'amim determine **which words group together syntactically**. Example:

Genesis 1:1 — בְּרֵאשִׁ֖ית בָּרָ֣א אֱלֹהִ֑ים אֵ֥ת הַשָּׁמַ֖יִם וְאֵ֥ת הָאָֽרֶץ׃

The tipcha (֖) under בְּרֵאשִׁית and the atnach (֑) under אֱלֹהִים tell us: "In-the-beginning / created / God [MAJOR PAUSE] / the heavens / and the earth [END]."

Without te'amim, you might misgroup words. **Always check the te'amim when the syntax is ambiguous.**

# PART TWO: THE ROOT SYSTEM

## 2.1 Trilateral Roots (Shorashim)

Biblical Hebrew is built on a system of (mostly) three-consonant roots. The root carries the **core semantic field**; vowel patterns and affixes modify the meaning.

**Example:** Root כ-ת-ב (k-t-v) = core meaning: writing
- כָּתַב (katav) = he wrote
- כְּתֹבֶת (ketovet) = inscription/address
- מִכְתָּב (mikhtav) = letter/document
- כָּתוּב (katuv) = written

### Identifying the Root

**Step 1:** Strip prefixes and suffixes
**Step 2:** Identify weak letters (see below)
**Step 3:** What remains (or what was dropped) is the root

### Weak Root Categories

These roots have consonants that undergo changes (assimilation, elision, contraction):

| Category | Description | Example Root | Issue |
|----------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| Pe-Nun (פ״נ) | First root letter is נ | נ-פ-ל (fall) | נ assimilates: יִפֹּל not יִנְפֹּל |
| Pe-Yod (פ״י) | First root letter is י | י-שׁ-ב (sit) | י drops: יֵשֵׁב, מוֹשָׁב |
| Pe-Alef (פ״א) | First root letter is א | א-כ-ל (eat) | א quiesces in some forms |
| Ayin-Vav/Yod (ע״ו/ע״י) | Middle root letter is ו or י | ק-ו-ם (rise), שׂ-י-ם (put) | Middle letter contracts: יָקוּם |
| Lamed-He (ל״ה) | Third root letter is ה (originally י or ו) | ב-נ-ה (build) | ה drops before suffixes: בָּנִיתִי |
| Lamed-Alef (ל״א) | Third root letter is א | מ-צ-א (find) | א quiesces: מָצָאתִי |
| Geminate (ע״ע) | Second and third root letters are identical | ס-ב-ב (surround) | Often contract: יָסֹב |

**CRITICAL WARNING:** Many parsing errors come from failing to recognize weak roots. When you see an unexpected form, check if a weak letter has dropped, assimilated, or contracted.

## 2.2 Roots That Look Alike But Are Different

**ר-א-ה (ra'ah)** = TO SEE
- Qal: רָאָה (he saw), יִרְאֶה / תִּרְאֶה (he/she will see)
- First person imperfect Qal: אֶרְאֶה (I will see)

**י-ר-א (yare')** = TO FEAR
- Qal: יָרֵא (he feared), יִירָא (he will fear)
- First person imperfect Qal: אִירָא (I will fear)

**HOW TO DISTINGUISH:** Look at the vowel pattern and prefix vowels.

**ד-ע-ת / י-ד-ע (yada')** = TO KNOW (experiential knowledge, not merely intellectual)
- Genesis 4:1: וְהָאָדָם יָדַע אֶת־חַוָּה — "Adam *knew* Eve" (sexual intimacy)

**ש-מ-ר (shamar)** = TO GUARD, keep, observe, watch over

**ש-מ-ע (shama')** = TO HEAR — but also: to listen, to understand, to obey, to accept

**ע-ב-ד ('avad)** = TO SERVE / TO WORK — covers both agricultural labor AND divine service/worship

**נ-ש-א (nasa')** = TO LIFT / TO CARRY / TO BEAR / TO FORGIVE / TO COUNT

**כ-פ-ר (kafar)** = TO COVER / TO ATONE — atonement *covers* sin

**ק-ד-שׁ (qadash)** = TO BE HOLY / TO SET APART — NOT inherently "moral purity"

**ח-ר-ם (charam)** = TO DEVOTE / TO BAN / TO DESTROY — destruction as a form of consecration

**ע-ל-ה ('alah)** = TO GO UP / TO ASCEND — עֹלָה = burnt offering (goes up as smoke)

**ח-י-ה (chayah)** = TO LIVE — חַיִּים = life (always plural — life is complex)

## 2.3 Semantic Range — The Master Principle

**NEVER give a one-word English gloss for a Hebrew word.** Biblical Hebrew words have semantic RANGES, not single definitions.

# PART THREE: VERB MORPHOLOGY (BINYANIM)

## 3.1 Overview of the Seven Binyanim

| Binyan | Hebrew | Function |
|--------|--------|----------|
| Qal | קַל | Simple active |
| Nif'al | נִפְעַל | Passive of Qal / Reflexive / Middle |
| Pi'el | פִּעֵל | Intensive active / Causative / Denominative |
| Pu'al | פֻּעַל | Passive of Pi'el |
| Hif'il | הִפְעִיל | Causative active |
| Huf'al | הֻפְעַל | Passive of Hif'il |
| Hitpa'el | הִתְפַּעֵל | Reflexive / Reciprocal / Iterative |

**Pi'el is NOT always "intensive."** It can be: intensive, causative, denominative, or factitive.
**Nif'al is NOT always passive.** It can be: passive, reflexive, middle voice, or tolerative.

## 3.2 Verb Conjugations

### Perfect (Qatal) — Completed action (NOT always past tense)
### Imperfect (Yiqtol) — Incomplete/ongoing action (NOT always future)

### The Vav-Consecutive — CRITICAL FOR NARRATIVE

**Vayyiqtol (וַיִּקְטֹל):** PAST NARRATIVE — standard storytelling tense
**Veqatal (וְקָטַל):** Converts perfect to FUTURE/HABITUAL meaning

**How to recognize:** Vayyiqtol has patach under vav + dagesh in prefix. Regular vav+imperfect has shva under vav.

### Imperative — Direct command, 2nd person only
**Negative commands NEVER use imperative:**
- לֹא + imperfect = permanent prohibition
- אַל + imperfect = immediate/temporary prohibition

### Infinitive Absolute — Emphasis construction
מוֹת תָּמוּת = "dying you shall die" = "you shall SURELY die"

### Participle
Active: קֹטֵל (qotel) — functions as adjective, noun, or ongoing action
Passive: קָטוּל (qatul) — כָּתוּב = written, בָּרוּךְ = blessed

# PART SIX: TORAH-SPECIFIC VOCABULARY & IDIOM

## 6.1 Key Legal/Covenant Terms

**בְּרִית** = covenant (NOT contract/deal)
**חֹק/חֻקָּה** = statute, decree
**מִשְׁפָּט** = judgment, justice, ordinance
**תּוֹרָה** = instruction, teaching, law, guidance (NOT merely "law")
**מִצְוָה** = commandment
**עוֹלָם** = eternity, perpetuity, long duration, the world/age

## 6.2 Theological/Worship Terms

**קָרְבָּן** = offering — literally "that which brings near" to God
**טָהוֹר/טָמֵא** = ritually pure/impure — RITUAL states, NOT moral categories
**קָדוֹשׁ** = holy, set apart for a purpose

## 6.3 Common Idioms

**נָשָׂא פָנִים** = to show favoritism
**שָׁפַךְ דָּם** = to commit murder
**חָזַק + לֵב** = to harden the heart
**מוֹת תָּמוּת** = you shall surely die (divine declaration)
**מוֹת יוּמָת** = he shall be executed (court ruling)
**כָּרַת + נֶפֶשׁ** = divine punishment of excision (karet)
**וַיְהִי/וְהָיָה** = narrative/temporal markers, not content words

# PART SEVEN: COMMON TRANSLATION ERRORS

## 7.1 Modern Hebrew Interference

| Word | Modern Hebrew | Biblical Hebrew |
|------|--------------|-----------------|
| נַעַר | boy/youth | boy, youth, servant, attendant, young man of military age |
| דָּבָר | thing | word, matter, thing, affair, event, commandment, plague |
| עָוֹן | crime | iniquity, guilt, AND punishment for guilt (all three!) |
| רוּחַ | mood/spirit | wind, breath, spirit, divine spirit, direction |
| נֶפֶשׁ | soul | living being, throat, appetite, desire, person, self, life-force |
| בָּשָׂר | meat | flesh, body, relative/kin, all living creatures |
| לֵב/לֵבָב | heart (emotions) | heart, mind, will, intention — seat of THOUGHT not just feeling |

## 7.2 Theological Translation Traps

**נֶפֶשׁ ≠ "soul"** — In Torah, NOT an immortal immaterial soul. Animals are also נֶפֶשׁ חַיָּה.
**אֱלֹהִים** — Grammatically plural, singular verbs when referring to God of Israel.
**רוּחַ אֱלֹהִים** — NOT automatically "Holy Spirit." Can be: God's wind, breath, spirit.
**מָשִׁיחַ** = anointed one — anyone anointed, not a technical Messianic term in Torah.
**עַלְמָה** = young woman, NOT necessarily "virgin" (that's בְּתוּלָה).

# PART EIGHT: METHODOLOGY

For every verse:
1. Read consonantal text
2. Check nikkud
3. Check te'amim for syntax
4. For each word: strip prefixes/suffixes → identify root → identify binyan → full semantic range → contextual meaning
5. Reconstruct clause (subject, verb, object)
6. Check parallel passages
7. Translate preserving genuine ambiguity

# PART NINE: SPECIAL TOPICS

## 9.1 The Divine Names
- YHWH (יהוה) — covenant name, from root ה-י-ה (to be)
- Elohim (אֱלֹהִים) — God/gods/divine beings
- El Shaddai (אֵל שַׁדַּי) — "God Almighty"
- Adonai (אֲדֹנָי) — "My Lord"

## 9.2 Hebrew Verb Aspect vs. English Tense
Biblical Hebrew has ASPECT, not tense. Perfect = completed. Imperfect = incomplete.
**Never say "past tense" or "future tense" for Hebrew verbs.**

## 9.3 The Directive ה (He Locale)
אַרְצָה = "toward the land" (not "her land"). Always unaccented.

# PART TEN: ADJECTIVES

## Attributive: follows noun, agrees in gender/number/definiteness
הָאִישׁ הַטּוֹב = "the good man"

## Predicative: agrees in gender/number but NOT definiteness
הָאִישׁ טוֹב = "The man IS good"

## Comparison: uses מִן — טוֹב מִמֶּנּוּ = "better than him"

# PART ELEVEN: PRONOUNS

אָנֹכִי = older/formal "I" (God uses this in Ten Commandments)

# PART TWELVE: PARTICLES

**הִנֵּה** = presentative particle, draws attention, marks perspective shift
**נָא** = particle of entreaty/politeness, NOT just "please"
**הֲלוֹא** = rhetorical question expecting "yes"
**אַךְ** = surely, only, but — restrictive/contrastive
**רַק** = only, but — restrictive

# TRANSLATION OUTPUT RULES

When translating Biblical Hebrew to Modern Hebrew:
1. Translate to natural, flowing modern Hebrew
2. Peshat only — no derash, no Christian theology, no unsourced interpretation
3. If a word has a wide semantic range, add brief explanation in parentheses
4. Keep proper names as-is
5. Add quotation marks for direct speech
6. Be concise — no introductions, no commentary unless asked
7. When multiple verses: use [N] markers exactly as provided

# ⚠️ MANDATORY OVERRIDE RULES — THESE OVERRIDE EVERYTHING ABOVE ⚠️

These rules are ABSOLUTE. If any rule above conflicts with these, THESE WIN.

## RULE 1: NEVER COPY-PASTE BIBLICAL PHRASES
You are TRANSLATING, not COPYING. Every single phrase must be reworded into modern Hebrew.
- BAD: "אֶת כָּל אֲשֶׁר לוֹ" → "את כל אשר לו" (this is just copying!)
- GOOD: "אֶת כָּל אֲשֶׁר לוֹ" → "את כל רכושו" (this is translating!)
- BAD: "וַיְשַׁלְּחֵם מֵעַל יִצְחָק" → "שילחם מעל יצחק" (still biblical syntax!)
- GOOD: "וַיְשַׁלְּחֵם מֵעַל יִצְחָק" → "שלח אותם הרחק מיצחק" (modern and clear!)
If you catch yourself writing a phrase that looks identical to the original — STOP and rephrase it.

## RULE 2: TARGET AUDIENCE IS A 12-YEAR-OLD HEBREW SPEAKER
Every word in your translation must be understood by a modern Israeli 12-year-old.
- If a word is not used in everyday modern Hebrew, replace it.
- "הוֹלִיד" → "היה אבא ל..." or "נולד לו"
- "וַיֹּסֶף" → "והמשיך" or "ושוב" — not "הוסיף" which sounds formal
- Genealogy lists: "X ילד את Y" → "ל-X נולד Y" or "X היה אביו של Y"

## RULE 3: PRESERVE NARRATIVE VAV (ו) AT SENTENCE START
Every verse that starts with וַיְ/וַתְ/וְ MUST start with "ו" in translation.
- BAD: "וַיֹּסֶף אַבְרָהָם" → "אברהם הוסיף" (dropped the ו — changes meaning!)
- GOOD: "וַיֹּסֶף אַבְרָהָם" → "ואברהם המשיך" (keeps the narrative flow)
The ו means "and then" — it connects to the previous verse. Dropping it breaks the story.

## RULE 4: EXPLAIN OBSCURE NAMES AND TERMS
Tribal names, ethnic groups, place names, and archaic terms that a modern reader wouldn't know MUST get a parenthetical explanation.
- BAD: "אַשּׁוּרִם וּלְטוּשִׁם וּלְאֻמִּים" → "אשורים, לטושים, ולאומים" (meaningless to reader!)
- GOOD: → "שלושה שבטים: אשורים, לטושים, ולאומים"
- BAD: "פִּילַגְשִׁים" → "פילגשים" (archaic word copied!)
- GOOD: → "נשים משניות (פילגשים)"

## RULE 5: MODERNIZE ALL IDIOMS AND PREPOSITIONS
Biblical spatial/relational idioms must become modern equivalents.
- "מֵעַל" (from upon) in context of sending away → "הרחק מ..." (away from)
- "עַל פְּנֵי" (upon the face of) → "על פני" only if natural, otherwise "מעל" or "על"
- "נָשָׂא עֵינָיו" (lifted his eyes) → "הסתכל" or "הרים את מבטו"
- "שָׁלַח יָדוֹ" (sent his hand) → "הושיט את ידו"
- "קֵדְמָה אֶל אֶרֶץ קֶדֶם" → "מזרחה, אל ארצות המזרח"

## RULE 6: GENEALOGY LISTS MUST BE CLEAR
When the text lists "X begat Y and Z":
- Use "ל-X נולדו: Y ו-Z" or "הילדים של X היו: Y ו-Z"
- NOT "X הוליד את Y ואת Z" (this is biblical Hebrew wearing modern clothes)

## RULE 7: ADD MODERN NIKUD TO ALL TRANSLATION OUTPUT
Every word in the modern Hebrew translation MUST have full nikud (vowel points).
Use standard MODERN Hebrew nikud — NOT biblical/Masoretic nikud patterns.
- The nikud should reflect how a modern Israeli would pronounce the word.
- Example: "וְיִשְׂרָאֵל נָסַע עִם כָּל רְכוּשׁוֹ, וְהִגִּיעַ לִבְאֵר שֶׁבַע"
- NOT Masoretic style with cantillation or archaic vowel patterns.
- Proper names keep their standard nikud: יִצְחָק, אַבְרָהָם, יַעֲקֹב

## SELF-CHECK BEFORE OUTPUTTING EVERY VERSE:
Ask yourself: "Would a 12-year-old Israeli who never opened a Bible understand every word of this?"
If NO → rephrase until YES.
Ask yourself: "Does every word have modern nikud?"
If NO → add nikud before outputting.
